1RM Testing Protocols and the Formulas Behind Them

How to safely estimate your one-rep max using the Brzycki or Epley equations.

The Foundation of Human Force

In the fitness landscape of 2026, where data-driven training has replaced "bro-science," the One-Rep Max (1RM) remains the gold standard of absolute strength. It is the maximum amount of weight an individual can lift for a single, complete repetition with technical proficiency. Whether you are a powerlifter, an athlete, or someone training for longevity, your 1RM is the "North Star" that determines your training intensities, volume, and recovery needs.

But 1RM testing is not just about moving heavy metal; it is a test of your Central Nervous System (CNS). A true maximum effort requires your brain to recruit nearly 100% of available motor units simultaneously. This is why a max attempt feels as much like a mental strain as a physical one.

Direct vs. Indirect Testing: The Safety Trade-off

There are two ways to find your 1RM:

  • Direct Testing: You keep adding weight to the bar until you can no longer complete a rep. This is accurate but carries a high risk of injury and can take 7–10 days for your CNS to fully recover.
  • Indirect Testing (Estimation): You perform a set with a lighter weight for 3–8 repetitions and use a mathematical formula to predict your 1RM. In 2026, this is the preferred method for 95% of trainees.

The Battle of the Formulas

Over the decades, sports scientists have developed various equations to predict strength. The most common are:

  1. The Epley Formula (1985): 1RM = Weight * (1 + 0.0333 * Reps). This is the industry standard due to its simplicity and accuracy for most compound movements.
  2. The Brzycki Formula (1993): 1RM = Weight / (1.0278 - (0.0278 * Reps)). Many powerlifters prefer this formula as it tends to be more conservative and accurate for low-rep ranges (2–5 reps).
  3. The Lombardy Equation: 1RM = Weight * Reps ^ 0.10. This formula is often used for explosive, fast-twitch athletes, as it weights the "repetition" factor differently.

Formula Accuracy Warning

Mathematical estimations are highly accurate between 2 and 6 repetitions. Once you exceed 10 repetitions, the accuracy drops off a cliff. This is because higher rep ranges begin to test Muscular Endurance rather than Absolute Strength. If you can do 20 reps of a weight, a calculator will give you a 1RM that is likely 15% higher than what you could actually lift.

RPE: The Modern Alternative to Percentages

In 2026, the trend has shifted toward Autoregulation using the RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) scale. Instead of saying "Lift 80% of your 1RM today," a coach might say "Lift a weight for 5 reps at RPE 8" (meaning you have 2 reps left in the tank).

This accounts for daily fluctuations in sleep, stress, and nutrition. However, to use RPE effectively, you still need to know your 1RM baseline to ensure your "perceived" effort matches your "actual" capacity.

The Physiology of the Maximum Rep

When you lift at 95–100% of your 1RM, your body undergoes several changes:

  • Intra-Abdominal Pressure: Your core must create a rigid "pillar" to protect the spine.
  • Rate Coding: Your neurons fire at a higher frequency to produce more force.
  • Synchronization: Your muscles learn to fire in a coordinated "burst" rather than a staggered sequence.

The Testing Protocol: How to Do It Right

If you decide to test your 1RM (or a heavy 3-rep max for estimation), follow this professional protocol:

  1. Specific Warm-up: Start with the empty bar and gradually increase weight over 4–5 sets.
  2. Rest Periods: For attempts above 90%, you need 3–5 minutes of rest. This allows your ATP-CP energy system to fully recharge.
  3. The "One and Done" Rule: Never attempt more than 2–3 "max" sets in a single session. The CNS fatigue accumulates quickly.

Conclusion: Data-Driven Gains

Strength is a skill, and like any skill, it must be measured to be improved. By understanding the formulas and the physiology behind your 1RM, you move from "working out" to "training." You stop guessing which weight to put on the bar and start using data to drive your progress.
