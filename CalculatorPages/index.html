The Problem With Online Calculators — And Why We Built Something Better

The internet has no shortage of calculators. Search for a mortgage calculator, a BMI tool, or a compound interest estimator and you'll surface dozens of results within seconds. The problem isn't quantity. The problem is quality, trustworthiness, and the experience of actually using them.

Most calculator sites were built during the early 2000s blogging era, updated occasionally to refresh advertising units, and never meaningfully improved. Their formulas are unattributed. Their mobile layouts are broken. Their results are surrounded by so many ads and pop-ups that extracting a number feels like navigating a tollway. And when you're trying to make a real financial decision — whether to refinance your mortgage, how much protein you need to hit your goals, whether an LLC or S-Corp structure will save you money — you need a tool you can actually trust.

YourCalc was built to solve this. One hundred and eleven tools across twenty-seven categories, all built to professional standards, all verified against primary sources, all running client-side in your browser so your data never touches a server. No accounts. No paywalls. No data stored. Just math, done correctly, when you need it.

What "Professional-Grade" Actually Means

It's a phrase that gets thrown around without definition, so let's be specific about what it means in practice. Every financial formula on this site — mortgage amortization, compound interest, FIRE number calculation, debt payoff modeling — uses the same equations that certified financial planners, mortgage underwriters, and actuaries use in professional practice. The mortgage amortization formula is the textbook standard. The 4% FIRE rule is derived from the original Trinity Study. The debt avalanche vs. snowball comparison uses the same methodology financial counselors use when working with clients.

Every health and clinical formula is sourced from published medical and exercise science literature. The Mifflin-St Jeor equation for BMR is the formula recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. The Cockcroft-Gault equation for creatinine clearance is referenced in the prescribing information of hundreds of FDA-approved drugs. WHO BMI classifications are used exactly as WHO publishes them. The US Navy body fat method is the same protocol used in military fitness assessments.

Engineering tools use standard electrical engineering formulas — Ohm's Law, three-phase power calculations, voltage drop equations — consistent with NFPA and IEEE reference materials. Scientific calculators implement verified chemistry formulas: molarity, dilution (C1V1=C2V2), the ideal gas law (PV=nRT). We don't invent formulas. We implement verified ones and show our work.

Client-Side Architecture: Why It Matters for Privacy

Every calculation on YourCalc runs entirely in your browser. This isn't a marketing claim — it's an architectural decision with real implications. When you use our mortgage calculator, your loan amount, income, and interest rate never leave your device. When you use the drug dosage calculator, the patient weight and medication concentration you enter exist only in your browser session. When you close the tab, everything is gone.

This stands in sharp contrast to most online tools, which route your inputs through servers, log them for analytics, and potentially use them for targeted advertising or data aggregation. We built client-side by default because we believe the right approach to privacy is simply not collecting data in the first place. There is no database to breach, no profile to build, no inputs to misuse.

Who Uses YourCalc

The user base is deliberately broad because the problems the tools solve are universal. Personal finance tools get used by first-time homebuyers trying to understand what a mortgage actually costs over thirty years, and by experienced investors stress-testing retirement scenarios. Health tools get used by competitive athletes optimizing training loads and by people starting a weight loss journey who want to understand their baseline metabolism. Clinical tools get used by nursing students in pharmacology courses and by practicing nurses verifying IV drip rate calculations at the bedside.

Small business tools get used by early-stage founders who have never built a financial model and by serial entrepreneurs who want a quick sanity check on unit economics. Engineering tools get used by electrical engineers validating field calculations and by DIY homeowners trying to understand whether their panel can handle a new load. The breadth is intentional — professional-quality tools shouldn't be gatekept behind software subscriptions or professional credentials. The math is the math. Everyone deserves access to it.
